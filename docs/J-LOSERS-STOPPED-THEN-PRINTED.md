## TL;DR вҖ” the three headline findings

1. **His "barely stopped" memory is partly recency bias вҖ” but the deeper truth is worse.**
   His **median loser exit was вҲ’42%**, not "barely." Only **7.4%** of losers were cut within
   вҲ’15%. He didn't mostly get shaken out on tiny pokes; he mostly **held losers down to вҲ’42%
   and *then* sold near the lows.** (Part A)

2. **"Then it printed" is REAL and EXACT.** After he exited, the underlying continued
   *meaningfully* his way (вүҘ0.25% вүҲ $1 SPY) on **67.9% of his losers**. The contract he'd just
   dumped went on to peak at **вүҘ2Г— his exit price on 21.4%** of losers, **вүҘ3Г— on 21 trades**,
   **вүҘ5Г— on 5 trades**, max **11.8Г—** _(EST)_. He was directionally right and exited at the
   wrong time on two-thirds of his red trades. (Part B)

3. **Mechanical exits are NOT a money-printer on the loser-only subset вҖ” and that's the honest,
   stronger result.** Replaying every loser under the engine's exits nets **+$3,212 _EST_**
   (вҲ’$44,236 вҶ’ вҲ’$41,024). Why so modest? **83% of his losers hit the вҲ’50% catastrophe cap
   anyway** вҖ” because his вҲ’42% median exit was already *close to* the cap. The engine's edge
   shows up in the **14% of losers that hit TP1 (+30%) first and trailed to breakeven**, booking
   **+$1,546 _EST_** out of trades he'd booked as losses. (Part C)

> **The real lesson isn't "the engine would have saved all his losers."** It's that **J's
> discretionary panic-sell at вҲ’42% вҖ” right before the bounce вҖ” is the behaviour the engine
> removes.** The convexity he described ("cheap contracts hitting $1000") is real, and it is
> *concentrated in exactly the trades he sold for pennies near the bottom.*

### B1. Did the underlying continue his way after he exited?

- **Median favorable underlying move after his exit: 1.73 SPY pts (~17 SPX pts).**

**On two-thirds of his losers, the move he was right about kept going after he bailed.**

## Doctrine tie-in & tuning implications

### This is the lived evidence behind CHART-STOP-PRIMARY / the вҲ’50% catastrophe cap (C2, C3)

We widened premium stops to a **вҲ’50% catastrophe cap** and made **chart-stops primary** precisely
because tight premium stops whipsaw 0DTE winners (lessons **C2** L51/55/64, **C3** L58/74/100/101/
112/136/148/149). J's losers are the **real-world proof**:

- He functionally ran a **tight discretionary stop at his pain threshold (вҲ’42% median)** and got
  shaken out вҖ” then **67.9% of the time the move continued and 21.4% of the contracts printed вүҘ2Г—.**
- A **structural chart-stop** (exit when price breaks the level that defined the thesis, not at an
  arbitrary premium %) would have kept him in the 14% of trades that recovered to +30%, instead of
  booking them as losses at the bottom.

### Tuning signals from his data

1. **Is even our chart-stop / вҲ’50% cap too tight?** On the loser-only subset, holding to вҲ’50%
   barely beats his вҲ’42% (+$3.2K) вҖ” but that's loser-conditioned. The **14% TP1-rescue rate**
   says the value is real where structure holds. **Recommendation:** keep the вҲ’50% cap as the
   *catastrophe* backstop, but the primary exit should remain **structural (chart-stop)**, not a
   tighter premium number. Do **not** tighten the premium cap toward his вҲ’42% вҖ” that's the exact
   level that whipsawed him.

2. **A "hold past the first adverse poke" rule is supported.** Median favorable move *after* his
   exit was 1.73 SPY pts (~17 SPX). Many exits happened on the first 5m red bar. The engine's
   "no exit on a single adverse bar inside structure" stance (chart-stop, not bar-stop) is the
   correct generalization of his data. Candidate: **explicitly require the thesis level to break
   on a *closed* bar before stopping**, rather than reacting to an intrabar wick (already the
   spirit of chart-stop-primary; this data quantifies the payoff).

3. **Late-day and big-size losers are the worst-managed (A3, A4).** Late entries panic to
   вҲ’52% to вҲ’62%; 3+ lots ride to вҲ’47% to вҲ’58%. The **15:50 time-stop** + **per-trade risk cap /
   min-3-lots / no-add-after-loss** (Rule 6 + Rule 4) directly target the two worst clusters.
   **L168/C31** is reconfirmed: sizing-up = worse loss discipline.

4. **The "set TP targets" half of J's quote is addressed by mechanical TP1/runner.** He said he
   "couldn't set TP targets." The engine's TP1 (+30% fallback) + runner (2.5Г—) is the literal fix;
   the 39 TP1-rescues in Part C are that fix working on his own losers.

### Bottom line for the firm

J quit because he **held losers to a deep loss, panic-sold near the bottom, and watched the move
continue** вҖ” on two-thirds of his red trades, with one-fifth of the dumped contracts going on to
print вүҘ2Г—. The engine cannot make a losing 0DTE thesis win, and this study is honest that
mechanical exits net only **+$3.2K _EST_** on the loser-only subset. **What the engine removes is
the specific, repeated, expensive discretionary error: the вҲ’42% panic-sell right before the
bounce.** That вҖ” plus holding *winners* to targets (measured elsewhere, not here) вҖ” is the
discipline he couldn't hold and the reason the engine exists.
