## Finding 1 — Filter 9 vetoes the home-run trades

The two big winners under historical regime:

| Date | Time | Rejection | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2026-03-20 | 09:45 | 656.25 | **+$704** (TP1 + runner hit 3× target) |
| 2026-03-27 | 11:50 | 641.00 | **+$606** (TP1 + runner hit 3× target) |

Neither fires under production rules. Both are clean rejection-then-breakdown patterns
where the entry bar wicked into a level and closed below — but did NOT close below
the Fast EMA on that same bar. Filter 9 requires `close < Fast EMA`, which only
becomes true 2-4 bars later, by which time:
- The entry premium is much higher (the move already started)
- The runner has less room to reach 3× target

**This matches the pattern J observed on 5/4** (and now caught at backtest):
> "Brief adverse move. SPY drifted slightly higher within a small range. The ribbon
>  stayed bearish-stacked despite the upward drift. *This is the test of conviction* —
>  bias right, trigger right, but price hasn't moved yet."
> ...
> "11:00 (the breakdown): SPY broke decisively down through the ribbon's middle band."

J entered at 10:27 (rejection) and the breakdown fired at 11:00. Filter 9 wouldn't
have entered until 11:00 — missing 33 minutes of premium gain.

**Recommendation (R-0030 candidate):** revisit filter 9. Either:
- (a) Drop the `close < Fast EMA` condition (entry on rejection alone, with other filters)
- (b) Soften to `close < Pivot EMA` (still a meaningful threshold, but less restrictive)
- (c) Make filter 9 OPTIONAL — pass if 5+ other filters strong, OR if filter 9 fires

Option (b) is probably the right balance. Worth weekly-review evidence before changing.

## Finding 2 — Filter 8 (VIX) is selective but appropriate

Filter 8 (`VIX > 17.30 AND rising`) eliminates 25 of 38 historical-regime trades — and
the 13 it leaves are concentrated in HIGH and MID IV regimes (no LOW regime trades
either way). The trades that survive filter 8 have a higher win rate (62% vs 50%).

**Verdict:** keep filter 8. It's working as designed — gating to elevated-fear regimes
where bearish setups have an edge. The higher win rate justifies the lower trade count.

## Finding 3 — Premium-stop hits are lopsided losers

| Exit reason | Production rules | Historical regime |
|---|---|---|
| TP1_THEN_RUNNER_BE_STOP | 7 (~$60 avg) | 16 (~$70 avg) |
| EXIT_ALL_LEVEL_STOP | 2 (~-$95) | 15 (~-$80) |
| EXIT_ALL_PREMIUM_STOP | 3 (~-$130) | 3 (~-$130) |
| TP1_THEN_RUNNER_TARGET | 0 | 2 (+$650 each) |
| TP1_THEN_RUNNER_TIME | 1 | 1 |

The "TP1 then runner BE stop" pattern is the dominant winning outcome — but its avg
profit ($60-70) is small. Meanwhile, the rare home-run runner trade is $600+. The
strategy depends on capturing those rare moves to justify the small wins.

**This means the W/L ratio failure is structural.** Most trades will be small wins
or moderate losses. Without the home-run runner, expectancy is barely positive.
Filter 9 currently prevents the strategy from being net-positive in a meaningful way.

## Finding 5 — Engine + simulator are sound

The validation pipeline:
- **EMA ribbon** matches the live Saty Pivot Ribbon within 0.11 cents (fingerprinted)
- **All 5 filter primitives** match chart-anatomy.md numerical definitions (19 unit tests pass)
- **Pricing** passes put-call parity within $0.01 (Black-Scholes correctness)
- **Simulator** correctly handles same-bar TP1+stop conflicts, theta decay, time stop
- **3 historical trades** reproduce direction with plausible P&L magnitude

Where the model diverges from reality:
- Pricing uses `IV = VIX/100` proxy (real ATM 0DTE IV is 0.5-1.5× VIX depending on regime)
- We use mid premium (real fills include bid-ask spread cost)
- Levels are auto-detected (J's discretionary judgments aren't captured)
- 5/1 doesn't fire (yfinance/TV ribbon mismatch at the entry — data fidelity)

These are documented and acceptable for "does the directional setup work?" questions.
For production fill accuracy, would need CBOE-grade option chain history.
