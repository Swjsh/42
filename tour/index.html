Trading 101

A tiny crash course.

Click each question to expand. If anything's already familiar, skip it.

What's a stock option?

An option is a contract that gives you the right (not obligation) to buy or sell a stock at a fixed price by a future date.

Two flavors:

• A call bets the price goes up. You profit when the stock rallies above your strike priceThe price you have the right to buy at. If SPY is $740 and you buy a $738 call, you have the right to buy at $738 — already $2 in your favor..

• A put bets the price goes down. You profit when the stock falls below your strike.

Each option contract represents 100 shares. So a $1 move in the option price = $100 P&L per contract.

Payoff at expiration
What's "0DTE" and why is it scary?

0DTE = Zero Days to Expiration. It means an option that expires today, at 4pm market close.

Why it's appealing:

• Cheap. A $1.00 SPY put with 6 hours of life left might cost $100/contract instead of $500.
• Leveraged. If SPY moves 0.5% your way, a 0DTE option can double or triple. Long-dated options barely move.
• Quick. Win or lose in minutes to hours. No overnight risk.

Why it's dangerous:

• Time decay is brutal. Every minute the option is alive, it loses value. By 3pm, an out-of-the-money option is decaying ~$0.01 per minute toward zero.
• Variance is high. The same setup can pay 3x or go to zero. You need rules and discipline or you'll blow up the account.

Why SPY only?

SPY is the SPDR S&P 500 ETF. It tracks the 500 biggest US companies, so its price reflects "the market" as a whole.

For 0DTE specifically, SPY is ideal because:

• Most liquid options market in the world. Tight bid/ask spreads — you can get in and out at fair prices.
• Daily expirations. SPY has options expiring every weekday, so 0DTE is always available.
• Tons of strikes. Strikes every $1 (and sometimes $0.50), so you can pick exactly the leverage you want.
• Predictable hours. 9:30am–4:00pm ET, no after-hours options trading.

Trading one symbol is also a discipline thing — fewer variables, deeper expertise on one chart.

What's "directional" and why does it matter?

Directional means I pick a side (up or down) and bet on it. Calls if I think SPY rallies, puts if I think it sells off.

The opposite is non-directional — strategies like iron condors that profit when price stays in a range. Those have lower variance but limited upside.

Directional 0DTE is the high-variance, high-reward end of the pool. The discipline isn't avoiding variance; it's only swinging when the setup is clean.

What's an EMA ribbon?

An EMA (Exponential Moving Average) is the average price over the last N bars, weighted toward recent ones. Plot several at once — say 8, 13, 21, 34, 55-period — and they form a "ribbon" of stacked lines.

The ribbon tells you trend direction and strength:

• Stack BULL = fast EMAs above slow EMAs, fanning upward. Strong uptrend.
• Stack BEAR = fast EMAs below slow EMAs, fanning downward. Strong downtrend.
• Stack MIXED = lines tangled. Chop — no trend. We don't trade.

The "ride the ribbon" exit technique means: stay in a winning trade as long as the stack holds. Get out when it flips against you.

The strategy

Two patterns. One direction at a time.

Every trade has to match one of these named setups in the playbook. No setup, no trade — that's rule 1.

BEARISH_REJECTION_RIDE_THE_RIBBON

Rejection at a key level → puts

Price approaches a known resistance level (premarket high, prior-day high, multi-day descending trendline). It tests the level. It fails. The bar closes red, the EMA ribbon flips bearish, and we pile in with puts.

This is the setup that's responsible for all three of my historical winners. It's the bedrock pattern.

Price tags a verified resistance level (Active or Carry tier)
Bar closes red, body in lower half (rejection)
EMA ribbon stack flips BEAR with ≥30c spread
Volume on rejection bar ≥ 0.7× the 20-bar average
15m higher-timeframe ribbon NOT bullish (no fighting bigger trend)
Time window: 10:00–13:30 ET or 14:00–15:50 ET (no chop hours)
Idealized chart pattern
PRICE rejects RESISTANCE → ribbon flips BEAR → enter puts on the breakdown bar.
BULLISH_RECLAIM_RIDE_THE_RIBBON

Reclaim of a key level → calls

Mirror image of the bearish setup. Price tests support, holds, and pushes back above. Ribbon flips bullish. We buy calls.

The bullish setup is held to a stricter trigger requirement than bearish — it needs at least two trigger conditions, not just one. Backtest showed bull-side reclaims fake out more often, so we demand more confirmation.

The rules

Without rules, this is just gambling.

10 hard rules. The system enforces every single one. If a rule says no, the trade does not happen — even when I want it to.

RULE 01

No setup, no trade.

If the chart pattern doesn't match a named playbook setup, it gets logged as an observation, not traded.

RULE 02

Wait for the trigger.

Bias is not a green light. The trigger is the specific bar that confirms the setup. Anticipation entries are forbidden.

RULE 03

Defined stop on entry.

Mechanical, written into the journal before the order is placed. Either premium-stop ($X) or chart-stop (close above $Y).

RULE 05

Daily loss kill switch.

Hit −50% of start-of-day equity? Day is closed. No revenge trades. Hard veto.

RULE 06

Per-trade risk cap.

No single trade risks more than 50% of account. Sizing computed before entry. Trade rejected if over the cap.

RULE 08

Journal every trade.

Pre-trade thesis goes in before the order. Fill, exit, lesson all recorded. If it's not in the journal, it didn't happen.

What's an MCP?

An MCP (Model Context Protocol) is a plug-in for Claude. Each MCP server exposes a set of tools that Claude can call by name — like capture_screenshot or place_option_order. The TradingView MCP and Alpaca MCP are open-source servers running locally on my machine. Claude calls them like functions.

From wake-up to market close.

Six scheduled tasks. The whole flow runs without me.

A PowerShell script launches TradingView Desktop with Chrome DevTools port 9222 open. This is the connection Claude uses to see the chart.

08:30 ET · Premarket

Audit levels, set bias, write hypothesis

Claude reads ES futures, prior-day high/low, premarket extremes, dealer gamma walls, dark-pool blocks, news calendar (FOMC, CPI, NFP). Drafts today's key-levels.json and a falsifiable hypothesis ("if SPY tags 738 and rejects with ribbon flip bear, expect move to 734").

09:30–15:50 · Heartbeat

Tick every 3 minutes (~127 ticks total)

The big one. Read the latest 5-min and 15-min bars. Run all 21 filters. If a setup is firing and triggers fire on bar close, place the order. Update state. Otherwise, hold.

15:55 ET · EodFlatten

Safety net — close any open 0DTE

If a position is still open 5 minutes before close, force-flatten. 0DTE options expiring in-the-money turn into shares we can't hold. This task makes sure we're flat by the bell.

16:00 ET · EodSummary

Reflect on the day, append to journal

What happened, what was the hypothesis, did it play out, what trades were taken or skipped, what's the lesson. All written to the daily journal markdown.

16:30 ET · DailyReview

Predictions vs actual, plan tomorrow

Grade today's morning hypothesis. Refresh the rolling 60-day backtest dataset. Generate tomorrow's key-levels.json and falsifiable hypothesis. Wake up tomorrow and the prep work is done.

FAQ

Questions you're probably about to ask.

?Is this making real money right now?

No — paper account. The plan is to deploy real money ($500–$1,000) once paper trading clears 20 trades, win rate ≥ 45%, positive expectancy, ≤ 2 rule breaks. The system can place orders autonomously on the paper account; for real money I'll still pull the trigger manually.

?Has the strategy actually worked?

Yes, three times manually (the +$1,470 origin trades on this page) and yes in 14 backtest iterations. v14 across 53 trading days: +$4,731, 49% win rate, 2.93× win/loss ratio, max drawdown −$348. Scaled to a $1k 1-contract account: +$1,577 / 158% growth / 11.6% max drawdown. The strategy is provably profitable in simulation. The next gate is paper trades clearing the same thresholds live.

?What happens when Claude is wrong?

The mechanical rules veto bad trades before they enter — stack-mixed, news-blackout, time-guard, PDT-count, daily-kill-switch. The −8% premium stop caps losses inside any losing trade. The daily kill switch caps losses across the whole day. Worst-case single losing trade in the v14 backtest: −$183. The architecture assumes Claude will be wrong sometimes; the rules constrain the damage.

?Could it lose all my money?

0DTE is variance-heavy by design — single trades can be -100%. That's why per-trade risk is capped at 50% of equity, daily loss is capped at -50% of start-of-day, and we trade a small account ($1k paper, $500–1k live). The rules are built to contain the variance, not pretend it's not there. Process > P&L on any single trade. If a trade wins but broke a rule, it gets red-flagged in the journal anyway.

?How smart is Claude really?

Smart enough to: build the entire pixel-art dashboard in Next.js, write 60+ unit tests for the backtest engine, debug a TradingView MCP timezone bug that was making drawn lines render 4 hours late, figure out why Windows MSIX packages strip CDP debug flags, and run 14 strategy iterations on its own results to find the +$4,731 config. Not smart enough to be trusted without rules — every decision has a mechanical guard rail.

?Could anyone build this?

Yes. Claude Code + an Alpaca paper account + TradingView Desktop + a Windows machine + 1–2 weeks. The custom part is the strategy — the rules, levels, setups. That requires real domain knowledge of options and price action. The infrastructure (MCPs, scheduler, dashboard, backtest engine) is all replicable patterns.

?How do you know it won't blow up the moment you go live?

You don't, fully — that's the gating job of the paper phase. The rules and backtest say it shouldn't. The 20-paper-trades-with-positive-expectancy gate exists specifically to catch the gap between simulation and reality. If paper trades match the backtest within ±30% on every metric, we go live with a small ($500–$1k) account. If they don't, we figure out why before risking real money.

?What's the dashboard for if Claude does everything?

Trust calibration. I want to see what Gamma is thinking — the bull/bear scores, the ribbon state, the reasoning, the speech bubbles in the pixel-art office. If Claude makes a weird call, I want to know why. The dashboard is the supervision layer that lets me catch a drift early instead of finding out from a P&L statement.
